## [The impact of AI-generated text on the Internet](https://ai-on-the-internet.github.io/)

**Summary**
Researchers from Imperial College London, the Internet Archive, and Stanford conducted an empirical study measuring how much of newly published web content is AI-generated or AI-assisted, and whether this has changed the character of internet text. By mid-2025, approximately 35% of newly published websites were classified as AI-generated or AI-assisted. The study tested six specific hypotheses about AI's negative effects on online text — two were confirmed empirically (language becoming less varied in vocabulary, and text becoming more positive in tone), while four others found no statistical support despite being widely believed by the public surveyed. The key finding is a significant gap between what people believe AI is doing to the internet and what the data actually shows.

**Key takeaways**
- 35% of newly published websites were AI-generated or AI-assisted by mid-2025 — a substantial share of new content.
- Only 2 of 6 hypothesised negative effects were confirmed: semantic contraction (narrowing vocabulary) and positivity shift (text becoming more uniformly upbeat).
- Public perception of AI's harms to internet quality significantly outpaces what empirical measurement supports — useful corrective before making policy or product decisions.

*Rare empirical measurement of a claim everyone makes but few quantify — the semantic contraction finding is particularly actionable for anyone evaluating AI-generated content quality.*
