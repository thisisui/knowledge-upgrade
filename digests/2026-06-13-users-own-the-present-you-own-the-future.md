## [Users Own the Present. You Own the Future.](https://www.dir14.com/articles/users-own-the-present-you-own-the-future)

**Summary**
This article makes a sharp distinction between two types of knowledge in product development: users know their current reality intimately, but product teams are responsible for imagining and building what does not yet exist. The problem it addresses is that user research is often treated as a source of product answers rather than a source of context — when you ask users "what should we build?", you get their current mental model, not a vision of what could be. The author warns that smarter, more experienced users make this trap worse, not better: a confident executive in a research session will give you a detailed feature request that feels like insight but is actually sophisticated noise. The alternative is to research users' underlying goals and frustrations (not their feature ideas), then make the product leap yourself — that decision and its consequences belong to the product team, not the research participant.

**Key takeaways**
- The more articulate and senior your research participant, the more convincing their feature prescriptions will sound — but confidence in a research session does not mean accuracy; treat all feature suggestions as symptoms to investigate, not solutions to implement.
- Distinguish between what users say they want (a stated request) and what they are actually trying to achieve (the underlying goal); the gap between these two is where most product opportunity lives, and stopping at the stated request produces only incremental improvements.
- New interfaces often test poorly at first simply because they are unfamiliar, not because they are poorly designed; test whether users can figure something out after repeated use, not whether their immediate reaction is positive.

**Importance** ★★★★☆ (4/5)
*Precise, actionable reframe of a common research failure mode — the "smarter users, more convincing wrong answers" insight alone is worth the read.*
