## [Self-Fulfilling Projects](https://www.davesresearch.com/self-fulfilling-projects/)

**Summary**
This essay names a recurring failure pattern in product and organisational work: the "self-fulfilling project" — an initiative that, like a self-fulfilling prophecy, makes itself seem important. Such a project is rooted in a compelling story about what is true rather than in the actual structure of the business or market, and it is protected by the authority of a senior sponsor and by "self-sealing logic" that reshapes itself whenever challenged. The author's key insight is that you cannot defeat such a project with outside facts, because it is not anchored in reality to begin with; arguing rationally from the outside just bounces off. Instead, the only way to make progress is to temporarily suspend your disbelief, accept the project on its own terms, and rebuild a concrete picture of the real situation from inside the language and logic of the story.

**Key takeaways**
- A self-fulfilling project references itself for importance and detaches from real internal capabilities and external opportunities — it survives on narrative plus the sponsor's authority, not evidence.
- Outside facts don't work against it because it was never grounded in facts; the harder the sponsor's seniority, the harder the narrative is to pierce.
- The workable move is to argue from inside: accept its premises temporarily, then reconstruct concrete structure (e.g. via Wardley Mapping) in the project's own language so the narrative becomes a discussable assertion rather than the leader's authority.
