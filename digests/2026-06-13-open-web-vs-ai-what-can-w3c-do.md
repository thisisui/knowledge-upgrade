## [Open Web vs AI: What Can W3C Do?](https://hidde.blog/web-ai-breakout/)

**Summary**
Hidde de Vries summarises a W3C Advisory Committee session where web standards professionals and AI companies discussed two concrete harms that AI systems are causing to the open web. First, AI crawlers — automated programs that download web pages to train AI models — generate so much traffic that they strain or crash servers, with the load compared to deliberate denial-of-service attacks; small publishers and Wikipedia bear these costs without compensation. Second, AI tools now summarise and answer questions directly, which means users never visit the original websites, cutting off the advertising and subscription revenue that publishers depend on to fund their work. The session explored what W3C (the international body that sets web standards) could do: broker agreements between AI companies and publishers, build open standards for AI agents so the web stays accessible rather than locked behind proprietary tools, and create payment and attribution systems so content creators are compensated when AI uses their work.

**Key takeaways**
- AI crawlers impose real infrastructure costs on web publishers today — server bills, bandwidth, and moderation work — with no payment or opt-in mechanism; W3C is exploring standards to make compensation and attribution technically possible.
- The "open agentic web" proposal argues that if AI agents must browse the web on users' behalf, those agents should use open standards rather than proprietary APIs, so any AI tool can navigate any site rather than creating closed ecosystems.
- Not everyone agrees the web is under threat: some participants noted that search engines scraped content for decades under similar objections, and AI agents genuinely help users navigate information overload — the debate is unresolved.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*Early-stage policy discussion, not actionable yet — but the infrastructure-cost and attribution problems it names are real and will force decisions within the next 1–2 years.*
