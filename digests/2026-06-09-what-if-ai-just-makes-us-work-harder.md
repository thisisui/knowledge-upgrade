## [What If AI Just Makes Us Work Harder?](https://timharford.com/2026/04/what-if-ai-just-makes-us-work-harder/)

**Summary**
This article asks a question that most AI coverage ignores: instead of giving us more free time, will AI tools simply cause us to do more work in the same amount of time — or even more hours than before? The author draws on research from UC Berkeley, where workers using AI reported feeling busier and less able to rest, not more relaxed. He compares this to what happened with previous productivity tools: email did not reduce communication, it multiplied it; PowerPoint did not save time on presentations, it caused people to spend hours making their own slides. The article warns that AI is likely to follow the same pattern unless individuals and organizations deliberately design limits around how and when it is used.

**Key takeaways**
- When a tool makes work faster, people tend to do more work rather than work fewer hours — this is a well-documented economic pattern. AI is likely to increase the total amount of work people produce, not reduce the hours they spend working, because each hour of work becomes more valuable, which makes people want to work more of them.
- Research on workers using AI found that they began taking on tasks they used to delegate to others, used AI during breaks (eroding recovery time), and felt more overwhelmed — not less — because the ease of doing more made it harder to justify stopping.
- Two practical responses: (1) organizations can design clear handover rules so that any qualified person can pick up the work, reducing pressure on individuals to always be available; (2) individuals can batch their AI requests into a scheduled list instead of interrupting themselves every time a question comes up, which preserves uninterrupted thinking time.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*A well-grounded warning backed by historical precedent and current research — worth reading before assuming AI will automatically improve work-life balance.*
