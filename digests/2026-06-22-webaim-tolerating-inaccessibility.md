## [WebAIM: Tolerating inaccessibility](https://webaim.org/blog/tolerating-inaccessibility/)

**Summary**
The 2026 WebAIM Million report found that the top one million websites now average 56.1 accessibility barriers per homepage, up from 51 in 2025 — meaning the web is getting less accessible despite growing awareness. Christopher Phillips argues that this is not primarily a technical or legal problem: it persists because disabled users' experience is easy for organisations to ignore. Drawing on the principle "every system is perfectly designed to get the results it gets," he contends that sustained change requires connecting digital accessibility work to the broader disability rights movement, not just improving developer tooling. The article also calls out cookie consent pop-ups as a largely unexamined accessibility barrier.

**Key takeaways**
- Web accessibility is worsening year-over-year despite more investment in tooling and compliance frameworks — the bottleneck is organisational priority, not knowledge.
- Cookie consent dialogs are a significant and underreported accessibility barrier, especially for users with motor or visual impairments.
- Meaningful progress requires connecting digital accessibility to disability rights as a movement, not treating it as a technical checklist.
