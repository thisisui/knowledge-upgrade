## [After automation](https://every.to/p/after-automation)

**Summary**
Dan Shipper, CEO of Every, runs a company of 30 people that uses AI extensively — and is still hiring humans. His explanation: AI makes expertise cheap, which floods markets with undifferentiated output, which raises demand for human judgment to distinguish good from mediocre. The bottleneck shifts up, not away. He also dissects why benchmark graphs mislead: once a model saturates a benchmark (scores 80-90%), researchers simply reframe the problem at a higher level of abstraction, creating an infinite ladder that never reaches "humans no longer needed." His most precise point: current AI systems have autonomy (they can execute tasks) but not agency (they don't want anything) — someone still has to decide what to do and whether the result is good.

**Key takeaways**
- Each wave of AI commoditising a skill creates demand for human judgment one level above that skill — automation creates more review and integration work, not less.
- "Chart psychosis": benchmark saturation doesn't mean capability is done, it means the frame shifts — the fractal pattern keeps human oversight structurally necessary.
- Agents have autonomy but not agency; the "framer" who decides what goal matters is still human and is the actual bottleneck.

*One of the clearer practical frameworks for understanding why AI hasn't eliminated jobs yet — and why the mechanism that prevents it is structural, not temporary.*
