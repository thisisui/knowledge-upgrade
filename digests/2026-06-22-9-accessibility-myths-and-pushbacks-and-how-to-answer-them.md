## [9 Accessibility Myths and Pushbacks and How to Answer Them](https://stephaniewalter.design/blog/9-accessibility-myths-and-pushbacks-and-how-to-answer-them/)

**Summary**
Accessibility — the practice of designing websites and software so that people with disabilities can use them — is frequently blocked in organizations by predictable objections: "disabled people don't use our product," "no one complained," "it's too expensive," or "it will ruin the design." This article by Stéphanie Walter, a UX designer, provides specific data-backed counter-arguments for nine of these objections. Key facts include: 16% of the world's population lives with significant disability; fixing accessibility problems during the design phase costs 6.5 times less than fixing them during development; and the disability market represents $13 trillion in annual disposable income. The article also provides a set of small, concrete starting actions designers can take without waiting for organizational buy-in, such as fixing contrast issues in mockups and documenting alt-text for icons.

**Key takeaways**
- "No one complained" is not evidence of no problem — users who can't access a site simply leave; analytics data structurally undercounts disabled users because it only captures people who successfully loaded the page
- The business case is strong and specific: $6.9 billion per year lost to competitors due to inaccessibility, $1 invested returns ~$100, plus legal risk from the European Accessibility Act and US ADA lawsuits
- Designers can start without permission: fix contrast in your own mockups, document alt-text for every icon you design, include disabled users in usability research — these cost nothing and compound over time
