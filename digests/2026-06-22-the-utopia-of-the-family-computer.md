## [The utopia of the family computer](https://mudmapmagazine.com/the-utopia-of-the-family-computer/)

**Summary**
This essay reflects on the era of the family desktop computer — a single shared device in a fixed location, used in scheduled shifts, treated as a household resource like a TV or telephone. The author argues that this model represented a form of technological containment: the internet had a physical place in the home, and going online was a deliberate act with a beginning and an end. The arrival of laptops and smartphones destroyed that containment model, making connectivity permanent and omnipresent. Drawing an analogy to the mechanical clock — which started as a local tool and ended up restructuring entire societies — the essay asks what was quietly lost when digital life lost its edges.

**Key takeaways**
- "Going online" was once a bounded activity with shared negotiation; it became invisible background infrastructure almost without anyone deciding it should
- The family computer desk was an artifact of containment — once the device became personal and portable, the containment was gone and couldn't be recovered
- Technological change that feels gradual and optional often reorganizes social structures permanently before anyone notices the shift
