## [Fast is Better Than Slow](https://dubroy.com/blog/fast-is-better-than-slow/)

**Summary**
This article argues that working speed is one of the most under-appreciated traits of effective programmers. Over a decade of observation, the author noticed that the best engineers consistently turn ideas into prototypes or fixes within hours of discussing them, and that this speed compounds: moving faster means you gather information sooner, learn faster, and can try several solutions and keep the best one. The author is careful to separate this from "hustle culture" — the point is working smarter to remove delay, not simply working longer hours. The piece then offers concrete habits to become faster, such as starting immediately instead of procrastinating, shipping work at roughly 70% done to get feedback earlier, and not over-building beyond what is actually required.

**Key takeaways**
- Speed creates a virtuous cycle: faster feedback → faster learning → more experiments → better decisions; it's a skill to cultivate, not just innate talent.
- Beat perfectionism with the "70% rule" (Oliver Burkeman) — publish work that's about 70% complete so feedback arrives sooner instead of polishing in isolation.
- Practical levers: don't procrastinate (delay rarely simplifies a problem), reclaim fragmented time, ask colleagues early instead of struggling solo, accept minor code-review nits quickly, and build only what's required.
