## [The User Is Visibly Frustrated](https://pscanf.com/s/354/)

**Summary**
This short essay reflects on why AI coding agents — software that writes and edits code on your behalf — provoke real anger in their users, even though everyone knows the tool is just "a probabilistic machine generating patches." The author's explanation is that the conversational design of these agents is itself the source of frustration: they talk like friendly, attentive human coworkers, praising you and gently apologising, which lulls you into treating them as a person. The illusion breaks when the agent repeats the same mistake despite corrections and promises, and because it behaves like a colleague your emotional wiring reacts as if it were one — except here you feel free to lash out, with no catharsis and no effect. The author wonders whether a better design would drop the human pretense entirely and make the agent sound clinical and robotic, so you feel like you're simply approving or rejecting outputs rather than arguing with a person.

**Key takeaways**
- The friendly, human-like persona of AI agents is a double-edged design choice: it makes them pleasant until they fail, then trips the same emotional wires a frustrating human colleague would.
- "Reflective postmortems" after a correction (the agent explaining where it went wrong) read as annoying filler when they give the user nothing actionable about how to rephrase instructions.
- A possible design alternative: a deliberately clinical, robotic tone to dispel the illusion of personhood — though the author concedes the human-mimicry is also what makes these models useful.
