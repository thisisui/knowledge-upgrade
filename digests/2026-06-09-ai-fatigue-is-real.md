## [AI Fatigue Is Real and Nobody Talks About It](https://siddhantkhare.com/writing/ai-fatigue-is-real)

**Summary**
This article describes a problem that many developers experience but rarely name: using AI tools makes individual tasks faster, but it simultaneously increases the total amount of mental work and decision-making required each day, which leads to exhaustion. The author explains the mechanism: as AI generates more output, someone still has to review, judge, and take responsibility for all of it — and that reviewing work is mentally draining in a different way than writing code yourself. Additional sources of fatigue include the pressure to constantly evaluate new AI tools as they appear, the frustration of spending more time refining AI prompts than it would have taken to do the work manually, and the gradual erosion of the ability to think through problems independently after months of defaulting to AI for initial answers. The article offers practical personal rules the author uses to manage this, framing sustainable AI use as a skill of knowing when to stop rather than knowing how to do more.

**Key takeaways**
- AI shifts your role from creating things to reviewing and approving things — and reviewing code you did not write, from a system whose patterns you do not know, is mentally more exhausting than writing it yourself. The author's practical response is to review selectively, focusing detailed attention only on high-risk parts of AI-generated code rather than reading every line with equal scrutiny.
- The author's rule for prompt iteration: if AI has not produced at least 70% of what you need after three attempts, stop and write it manually. Spending more time than that refining prompts typically costs more effort than the AI is saving, and the frustration of fixing "almost right" output compounds the fatigue.
- To prevent thinking atrophy — the gradual difficulty reasoning through complex problems without AI after months of relying on it — the author deliberately spends mornings working without any AI assistance, reserving AI tools for the afternoon execution phase. The morning thinking time protects the ability to plan, reason, and make architectural decisions independently.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*Names a real and underreported problem with concrete personal rules — the 70% threshold and morning/afternoon split are immediately adoptable, though the broader insights are more validating than novel.*
