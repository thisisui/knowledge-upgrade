## [The Beginning of Programming as We'll Know It](https://bitsplitting.org/2026/04/01/the-beginning-of-programming-as-well-know-it/)

**Summary**
This article argues that AI coding assistants are impressive but produce low-quality code more often than the technology's enthusiasts admit — the failures are rarely talked about, while the successes are celebrated loudly, which creates a false picture of how reliable AI coding actually is. The author's position is that human programmers are still essential right now, not despite AI, but because of it: someone with good judgment needs to review, correct, and take responsibility for the code the AI generates before it is used in a real product. The article compares AI to a very confident but often wrong advisor — it will give you a plausible-sounding answer even when the answer is incorrect, which is dangerous if you accept suggestions without checking them. The author's recommendation is to use AI as a tool that speeds up your work while keeping yourself in control of the final result, rather than letting the AI drive and just approving whatever it produces.

**Key takeaways**
- Most AI-generated code is not good enough to use without a human reviewing it first. The reason AI seems more capable than it is, is that developers share and celebrate the cases where it works well, while quietly fixing or ignoring the many cases where it produces wrong or low-quality results.
- AI coding tools are dangerous in a specific way: they generate answers that sound correct and confident even when they are wrong. Unlike a compiler error that tells you clearly something is broken, bad AI code may look reasonable at a glance — which means you need to read and understand the code yourself before accepting it.
- The developers who will benefit most from AI are those who use it to move faster while keeping their own judgment in charge of the final product. Those who hand over full control to the AI will produce lower quality work; those who refuse to use AI at all will be slower than those who use it carefully.

*A grounded counterpoint to AI hype — useful for calibrating expectations, but the core message (review AI code carefully) is already well understood by experienced developers.*
