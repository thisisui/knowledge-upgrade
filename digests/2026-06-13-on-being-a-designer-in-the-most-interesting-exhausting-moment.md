## [On Being a Designer in the Most Interesting, Exhausting Moment of Our Careers](https://medium.com/design-bootcamp/on-being-a-designer-in-the-most-interesting-exhausting-moment-of-our-careers-7284735e0927)

**Summary**
Pawel Klasa describes four simultaneous pressures reshaping what it means to be a designer in 2026: designers must now understand AI-adjacent technical concepts (like machine-readable design tokens and LLM prompting); they must produce more distinctive work because AI generates "good enough" results for free, raising the quality floor; they must think strategically about entire product experiences rather than just individual screens; and they must deliver strategy documents and governance playbooks instead of Figma files. Klasa does not pretend these pressures are easy — he names the exhaustion honestly, including the tedium of reviewing AI-generated output and the identity strain on visual designers pushed into technical roles. The article draws parallels to desktop publishing in the 1980s, when the same kind of disruption sorted designers who adapted from those who did not, and argues that taste and judgment — knowing which AI output is worth keeping — are now the scarcest and most valuable designer skills.

**Key takeaways**
- Design systems must now serve two customers at once: human designers who read them and AI tools that consume them as machine-readable instructions — building only for humans means AI cannot use your system reliably.
- "Problem framing" (deciding which problem is worth solving) matters more than execution skill now that AI can execute quickly — the designer who defines the right question beats the one who answers the wrong one faster.
- Exhaustion from constant tool churn and QA-style AI review is real and named; sustainable practice requires choosing which new tools to learn deeply rather than skimming all of them.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*Honest practitioner-level account of AI's real effect on design work — more grounded than most takes, but stays at diagnosis rather than providing concrete new techniques.*
