## [The Death of Design](https://nathanbeck.eu/essays/death-of-design/)

**Summary**
Nathan Beck pushes back against the claim that AI tools are eliminating the need for designers. His central argument is that AI produces outputs that are "average, tidy, and predictably banal" — useful as a starting point, but incapable of the critical thinking that real design requires. The essay specifically challenges the idea that chat-based interfaces (where you type a request and the AI responds in text) will replace traditional software interfaces: Beck points out that these text-first interfaces exclude users with typing difficulties, speech impediments, or those in public spaces where speaking aloud is not appropriate. The real work of design, he argues, is translating a software system's internal logic into something diverse users can understand — and generative AI actually creates more design decisions to make, not fewer, because every AI-generated screen or flow still needs a human to judge whether it is correct, accessible, and appropriate.

**Key takeaways**
- Chat-based interfaces (where interaction happens entirely through typed or spoken conversation) are not a universal improvement — they actively exclude users who cannot type quickly, have speech difficulties, or are in environments where talking aloud is impossible; designing for these users still requires traditional interface thinking.
- Generative AI creates more design work, not less: every AI-generated layout, flow, or component still needs a human designer to evaluate whether it serves the actual user, fits the brand, and meets accessibility standards — the volume of decisions increases even if the time to produce a draft decreases.
- Design's value is in the conscious coupling of *how* (the interface mechanism) and *why* (the user's actual goal) — this critical judgment, which involves questioning organizational assumptions, cannot be automated away even when the visual output of design can be generated quickly.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*Well-reasoned rebuttal to "AI kills design" takes, with the chat-interface accessibility critique being the most concrete and underappreciated point — but covers familiar ground overall.*
