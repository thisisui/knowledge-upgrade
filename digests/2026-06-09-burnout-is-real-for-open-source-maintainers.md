## [Burnout Is Real for Open Source Maintainers](https://openjsf.org/blog/burnout-is-real-for-open-source-maintainers)

**Summary**
This article is an interview with John-David Dalton, the creator of Lodash — a JavaScript library (a collection of reusable code functions) that is downloaded over 100 million times per day and has been in use for more than a decade. The interview focuses on his experience of burnout: a state of physical and mental exhaustion caused by sustained overwork, which caused him to significantly reduce his contributions to the project for approximately five years. He describes how personal life events compounded the pressure, and how the constant fear of losing relevance if he stopped working made it harder to step back even when he needed to. The article also describes the structural changes the OpenJS Foundation helped implement — distributing maintenance responsibilities across a team and establishing a dedicated security group — so that the project no longer depends on a single person's availability.

**Key takeaways**
- A major source of burnout for open source maintainers is the psychological fear that stopping contributions will cause them to lose their community standing and relevance — Dalton found this fear was unfounded, as community trust remained intact during his years away, but the fear itself was real and made it harder to take necessary breaks.
- Recovery took approximately five years and required therapy, exercise, and deliberately separating programming from personal leisure time. The lesson is that sustainable contribution requires treating programming as a job with boundaries, not an identity that fills all available time.
- The practical fix for projects is governance: instead of one person being responsible for everything, establish a steering committee, a dedicated security team, and shared ownership of the codebase. This means the project can continue functioning when any individual maintainer is unavailable, sick, or overwhelmed.

**Importance** ★★☆☆☆ (2/5)
*Humanizing but not actionable beyond well-known advice — the structural governance recommendations are the most concrete part, but they are already standard practice guidance for open source projects.*
