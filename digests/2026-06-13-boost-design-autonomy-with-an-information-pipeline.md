## [Boost Design Autonomy with an Information Pipeline](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/information-pipeline/)

**Summary**
Sara Paul (Nielsen Norman Group) reports on research into why some designers gain real influence over product decisions while others remain stuck executing requests from other teams. The core finding is that designers who earn strategic influence treat information-gathering as a deliberate, ongoing system — not as something that happens passively. The article calls this system an "information pipeline" and breaks it into four parts: actively collecting data from across the organisation (analytics, support tickets, roadmaps), building relationships with people who hold relevant knowledge, creating regular shared spaces where teams exchange information, and combining multiple data sources to form credible recommendations. The article includes a concrete tracking template and estimates the time cost: a few hours to set up, one hour per week to maintain.

**Key takeaways**
- Design influence is built through trustworthy, well-informed recommendations, not through assertiveness alone — the research shows this credibility takes months or years to establish and requires consistently maintained information sources.
- An information tracker (a simple spreadsheet with columns for project, owner, files, status, and notes) updated weekly after meetings helps spot cross-team connections and identify problems that have already been solved elsewhere, avoiding duplicate work.
- Archiving outdated information rather than deleting it keeps the tracker usable over time — deleted context is often needed again when a project resurfaces or a new team member joins.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*Practical, research-backed framework for designers who want more product influence — actionable but requires sustained effort over months, not a quick fix.*
