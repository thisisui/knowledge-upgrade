## [Designing for AI Means Designing Like It's 1999](https://uxdesign.cc/designing-for-ai-means-designing-like-its-1999-da9c53d24644)

**Summary**
This article argues that designing AI products today feels like designing for the web in 1999 — a moment when nothing was settled and every convention still had to be invented. The author draws several parallels: the technical standards keep changing underneath you (he points to the Model Context Protocol shifting hands while teams build on it), no interface paradigm has won yet (chat became the default by accident, while voice, ambient agents, and embedded AI are all still being tried), and the underlying models improve so fast — capabilities roughly doubling every seven months — that careful design choices built around a model's current weaknesses quietly become obsolete. He also notes the shaky economics (AI labs lose money on consumer subscriptions, much like the venture-subsidised internet of 1999) and the gap between hype and reality (citing an MIT study finding 95% of AI pilots delivered no measurable impact). The central message is that the real design work right now is inventing conventions, not applying them.

**Key takeaways**
- Treat current AI design as pre-standardisation: the protocols, interface paradigms, and economics are all unsettled, so don't over-invest in conventions that will likely be overturned.
- Models improve roughly every seven months, so designs built specifically to compensate for a model's weak spots have a short shelf life — design for a moving target, not the model in front of you.
- The hardest design problem is not handling obvious failures but designing around outputs that are confidently wrong; chat is a temporary container, not the "killer app."
