## [Design from the Inside](https://mattstromawn.com/writing/design-from-the-inside/)

**Summary**
This article argues that traditional design — where a designer produces plans, specifications, or prototypes and then hands them off to engineers — breaks down in fast-moving product teams where the product changes faster than any document can keep up. The author proposes "inside-out" design: the designer works directly in the product's codebase, making small, visible changes (like placing tape on the floor to redirect foot traffic) rather than drafting blueprints that get stale before implementation. The practical shift is to stop creating design artifacts in separate tools and instead push changes directly to the product, letting the team adapt to the improved reality rather than approving a proposed future state. The article frames this not as skipping design thinking, but as compressing the distance between the design decision and the shipped result.

**Key takeaways**
- Work in the codebase instead of a separate design tool: use real data, real components, and real constraints so the gap between "design" and "shipped" collapses and decisions stay grounded in what actually exists.
- Ship for review instead of seeking pre-approval: push a code change and ask "does this work?" rather than presenting a mock-up and asking "should we build this?" — the team reacts to something real, not a hypothetical.
- Replace process documents with repeated behaviors: instead of writing guidelines or running workshops, model the design behavior you want (e.g., checking support tickets before speccing a feature) until it becomes a shared team habit.

**Importance** ★★★★☆ (4/5)
*Directly actionable for designers with coding ability — reframes the designer's job as continuous in-product iteration rather than a planning-and-handoff cycle.*
