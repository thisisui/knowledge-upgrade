## [AI Job Grief](https://jackmaguire.org/blog/ai-job-grief/)

**Summary**
This essay describes a psychological experience that many technology workers are having but that has no widely recognised name: a form of grief over losing their jobs, or fearing they will, because of artificial intelligence. The author's central point is that for knowledge workers, expertise is not just a skill but part of their identity — so when AI threatens the work, it threatens the person's sense of self, not only their income. He argues this grief is "disenfranchised": because layoffs are framed as ordinary business restructuring rather than a real loss, society gives these workers no permission to mourn, so the feeling leaks out sideways as anxiety, anger, and even workplace sabotage. The piece also notes this displacement is unusual because it is fast (years not generations), targets cognitive professionals, is a deliberate corporate choice, and has no clear endpoint to adapt toward.

**Key takeaways**
- For knowledge workers, professional skill is felt as identity, so AI displacement is experienced as symbolic loss of self, autonomy, and future — not merely lost pay.
- Because the loss is socially unacknowledged ("just restructuring"), the grief is denied an outlet and re-emerges as anxiety, anger, and sabotage (the author cites 44% of Gen Z employees admitting to undermining AI rollouts).
- Unlike past industrial shifts, this one is compressed in time, deliberately chosen, and open-ended — and the retraining/safety-net systems that once cushioned disruption have been weakened, leaving no shock absorber.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*A useful naming of a real and under-discussed emotional reality for tech teams and leaders, though more diagnosis than solution.*
