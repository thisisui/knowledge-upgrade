## [On frictionmaxxing and the creative virtues of inconvenience](https://www.itsnicethat.com/articles/pov-friction-breeds-creativity-creative-industry-260526)

**Summary**
Modern products are designed to remove friction — instant boiling-water taps, food delivery, AI summaries, Slack reactions in place of conversations — collapsing the gap between wanting something and getting it. This essay argues that in stripping out the waiting, wandering, and idle pauses, we also remove the very moments where creativity, intimacy, and a coherent inner life take shape. It introduces "frictionmaxxing," an emerging cultural reaction in which people deliberately choose inconvenience: going to a shop instead of ordering online, walking without headphones, writing something themselves rather than outsourcing it to AI. The deeper claim, drawing on philosopher Byung-Chul Han, is that smoothness has become so total that we now expect efficiency not just from machines but from relationships, careers, and emotions — and that a completely frictionless life ends up feeling numb rather than free.

**Key takeaways**
- The inefficiencies we try to eliminate — the kettle wait, the walk to the shop, boredom — are often exactly where ideas, memory, and connection actually arrive.
- Convenience has reset our expectations of life itself: we now treat discomfort as a design flaw and expect frictionlessness from dating, friendship, and self-improvement, none of which optimise.
- The practical move isn't rejecting convenience wholesale but becoming selective — keep removing trivial frictions, but protect the ones that are quietly essential to thinking and being human.
