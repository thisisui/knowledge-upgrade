## [The Machines Are Fine. I'm Worried About Us.](https://ergosphere.blog/posts/the-machines-are-fine/)

**Summary**
This article is about a danger in how AI is being used in academic research and professional learning: when people use AI to produce results without actually understanding the work themselves, the output looks correct but the person learns nothing. The author illustrates this with two imaginary PhD students who submit identical research papers — one student did the hard thinking herself, the other delegated it entirely to an AI. Their papers are indistinguishable on paper, but only one student actually developed the ability to think independently, catch errors, and do new work in the future. The article argues that the dangerous part is not a dramatic collapse, but a slow, gradual process where people accumulate credentials and experience without accumulating real understanding — and neither they nor their institutions notice until years later. This applies beyond academia: anyone who uses AI to skip the hard parts of learning a skill is trading short-term speed for long-term capability.

**Key takeaways**
- AI can produce output that looks correct to an outside observer but contains subtle errors — and the only reliable way to catch those errors is to already understand the subject well enough to notice something is wrong. An experiment described in the article shows Claude producing physics calculations with plausible-looking but fabricated numbers that were only caught because the supervisor had decades of experience in the field.
- Struggling with difficult problems — debugging code that does not work, reading papers that are hard to understand, working through failed calculations — is not a frustrating obstacle to learning; it is the actual mechanism by which learning happens. Skipping that struggle by asking AI for the answer means skipping the part that builds real understanding.
- The right way to use AI is to build foundational knowledge first through hard work, and then use AI to accelerate work you already understand. Using AI before you have that foundation means you cannot judge whether what it produces is correct, and you will not develop the judgment you need to work without it.

**Importance** ★★★★☆ (4/5)
*The framing of "gradual cognitive outsourcing" is precise and important — a useful lens for anyone managing how they or their team uses AI for learning-heavy work.*
