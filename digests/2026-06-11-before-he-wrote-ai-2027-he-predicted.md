## [Before He Wrote AI 2027, He Predicted 2026](https://asteriskmag.substack.com/p/before-he-wrote-ai-2027-he-predicted)

**Summary**
In 2021, Daniel Kokotajlo — an AI safety researcher who later co-authored the influential "AI 2027" report predicting rapid AI capability gains — wrote a detailed narrative essay called "What 2026 Looks Like," imagining the AI landscape five years out. This Asterisk Magazine interview reviews those 2021 predictions against what actually happened by 2026. Kokotajlo got several things right: AI companies hitting $2B+ revenue by 2023, US-China competition over advanced chip exports, and the rise of multi-agent architectures where AI systems coordinate and review each other's work. He missed the timeline on new semiconductor factories and overestimated AI-driven political polarization. The interview uses this track record as a springboard to ask whether his AI 2027 predictions — which include an "intelligence explosion" scenario where AI rapidly accelerates its own development — should be taken seriously, and how much weight a good historical track record should carry.

**Key takeaways**
- Narrative forecasting — writing out a concrete, detailed story of how the future unfolds step by step — surfaces logical inconsistencies and implausibility that abstract probability estimates miss; it forces you to commit to a causal chain rather than a vague direction.
- Kokotajlo's observation that "sensible moderates keep getting wrecked" on AI is a data point worth sitting with: the people who predicted slower, more cautious AI progress have been consistently wrong over the past decade, which should shift your prior toward taking fast-progress scenarios more seriously.
- His 2027 predictions involve more speculative transitions (AI learning continuously from deployment, new training architectures, recursive self-improvement) than his 2026 predictions did — historical accuracy on the simpler predictions does not straightforwardly transfer to confidence in the harder ones.

**Importance** ★★★☆☆ (3/5)
*A grounded retrospective on AI forecasting methodology — the track record matters less than the lesson that narrative specificity beats vague probability estimates.*
